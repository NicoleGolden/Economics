To start, in Chapter 1, I'd like to analyze the milk consumption patterns in the context of US migration.

Motivation: Why Milk Consumption?

  • Dairy milk: consistent decline
    • 1970: 1 cup daily per capita
    • 2019: \( \frac{1}{2} \) cup (-50%)
  • Plant milk: partly contribute to the decline
    • Price premium
    • Lactose-free, nutrient substitute, environmentally friendly, animal welfare, COVID-19


Source: USDA (2022), Loh, Seah and Looi (2021), and Foods Association (2022).

So why milk consumption? According to USDA, there's a consistent decline in dairy milk consumption since the 1970s. For example, in 1970, the daily per capita consumption in dairy milk is about 1 cup. By 2019, the daily per capita consumption reduced to half. Plant-based milk partly contributes to the decline, despite its price premium. People may consume plant-based milk as a nutrient substitute, and it's considered as more environmentally friendly and better for animal welfare. During covid-19, there was an increasing trend in consuming plant-based milk for reasons such as health or change of lifestyle. This makes the study of consumption for both types of milk important.

Research Question

  • How does migration to another state affect dairy & plant milk consumption?
    • Expenditure patterns
    • Geographic variations
  • Contribution:
    • Research gaps → quantify
    • Dietary policies: WIC
And brings to my research question: How does migration to another state affect dairy & plant-based milk consumption? Specifically, my research uncovers the expenditure patterns for both types of milk, and learns how the new local environment affects HH’s expenditures. This research fills in the current research gaps and quantifies these questions. It also has dietary related policy implications, especially to the policy for Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children, or known as WIC.

Background: Dairy vs Plant Milk

Source: Plant Based Foods Association (2020), Gerliani, Hammami and Aider (2019), and Boaitey and Minegishi (2020).
Image source: author.

Here's a comparison of dairy and plant-based milk. In terms of sales growth, dairy milk expericed only 0.1% increase in 2019, whearas it's 5% for plant-based milk. Traditional dairy milk contains lactose. It can be made lactose-free of course. But plant-based milk is inherently lactose-free. Dairy milk is a type of conventional beverage because it's been around since the 19th century. Whearas plant-based milk is a type of functional beverage. It's designed not only to satisfy our desire for milk-like taste but also nutritional needs. Lastly, plant-based milk has many other advantages in this modern world: such as lower environmental impact, as you can see the figures here; its production doesn't involve animals; it satisfies flexitarian lifestyle, so if you're vegan or allergic to dairy milk, plant-based milk is a more suitable alternative. During covid-19, many people also started to consume plant-based milk instead of just dairy milk.

DID Model: Geographic Variations

    \[ ln(y)_{it} = \alpha_{i} + \tau_{t} + \beta \cdot \Delta_{i} \cdot D_{it} + X^\prime_{it}\Gamma + \epsilon_{it} \]
  • \( \Delta_{i} = \bar{y}_{N,i} - \bar{y}_{O,i} \)
    • Size of move
    • Diff between average milk expenditures in mover's destination state vs origin state
  • \( \beta \) = Our interest
    • Measures how much milk expenditures converge towards destination states’ average

Source: Finkelstein et al. (2016).

This DiD model looks similar to the standar DiD model earlier but with an added \( \Delta_{it} \) term. It's called "size of move", which is the difference between average milk expenditures in mover's destination state vs origin state. By interacting "size of move" with treatment "post_move", we get \( \beta \), our interest. It measures how much milk expenditures converge towards destination states’ average.
(Note: this model uses movers data only.) (Note: "size of move" is calculated non-movers data only. To compute state-level average outcomes: first average across HHs in each quarter and in each state using sample weights (and info on number of adults), then take average across time periods. You can simply think of this as expenditure of self regress on expenditure difference of others (who are in your destination or origin states))
(Note: "beta" measures the fraction of difference between destination and origin that has been covered. This way, the size of jump after move measures share of average difference between areas that is attributable to current environment.)

Why should we care about financial health? According to Financial Health Network, an NGO that has been studying financial health of Americans for the past dacade, an estimated 67% of Americans are not financially healthy. Let me explain. Financial Health Network used the 2020 survey data of 6,430 respondents conducted by University of Southern California, and found that, on average, 37% of individuals spend more than their income or can't pay bills on time, 47% of them don't have sufficient liquid savings or long-term savings, 38% have unmanageable debt or non-prime credit, and 42% don't have appropriate insurance or don't plan ahead financially. In my study, I'd like to focus on the group who have non-prime credits. The non-prime consumers tend to take out payday loans. According to CFPB, there are 12 million payday loan users per year. People are attracted to borrow payday loans because they are typically small-dollar, short-term, and unsecured. However, payday loans are very costly. Typically the APRs are between 300% to 500%. As of 2020, 22 states either banned or effectively banned payday loan, because they see payday loan as predatory loans. Among the rest of states where payday loan is still legal, 15 states passed so called extended payment plans to protect payday loan borrowers.

In my reserach, I'd like to answer this question: Do extended payment plans improve financial health? A similar research is done by Wang and Burke (2022). They study the effects of information disclosure in Texas starting 2012 for payday loan consumers. The disclosure compares the cost of payday loans vs other credit products, and presents their likelihood of renewal in simple terms. They found that the disclosures have led to a significant and persistent decline in payday loan volume. In my study, the extended payment plan is a much broader payday loan policy (since they typically require more than information disclousre) and it's applied to multiple states (instead of just Texas).

Background: Payday Loans

  • Legal in 29 states (2020), small-dollar
  • Loan limit $500, unsecured, repay in next payday
  • High APRs: 300% ~ 500% (credit cards: 12% ~ 30%)
  • Features: "cash, quick, fast, easy, emergency bills"
  • Rollover: charges will double! → "debt traps"


Source: CFPB (2021).

Let me introduce payday loan a bit more here. It's a small-dollar loan with a typical loan amount of $500. It's legal in 29 states as of 2020. It's unsecured because it doesn't require any collateral and it doesn't require formal credit check. Consumers repay in their next payday. Compared with only 12~30% of APR, the payday loan has a much higher APR, typically between 300~500%. Based on advertisements I scrapped from the top 10 payday loan lender's websites, the payday loan is typically featured as being "quick cash, fast, easy, for emergency bill." If the user opts in rollover, which means they choose to pay back in the next payday instead of this one, the charges will double. The rollover feature of the payday loan keeps some consumers in "debt traps."

The plans may be limited to use to once a year. Consumers may only be eligible if they read a threshold of rollovers. The lends may be required to disclose the availability of the extended payment plans.

Pre-check: Event Study

Note: Following Sun and Abraham (2021), the model is \( y_{it} = \alpha_{i} + \beta_{t} + \sum\limits_{g}\sum\limits_{r \neq -1} \mu_{g,r} \left(\textbf{1}{(G_{i}= g)} \times event\_time_{it}^{r}\right) + \epsilon_{it} \).

We can also do an event study like in Chapter 1. We can see that there are various degrees of decrease in each outcome variable after the treatment went effect.

Model: SDiD

    \[ \hat{\tau}^{SDID} = argmin_{\mu, \alpha, \beta, \tau} \Bigl\{\sum_{i = 1}^{N} \sum_{t = 1}^{T} (Y_{it} - \mu - \alpha_{i} - \beta_{t} - \tau D_{it})^{2} \hat{w}_{i} \hat{\lambda}_{t} \Bigl\} \]
  • \( Y_{it} \) = Financial health in state \(i \), quarter \(t \)
  • \( \mu \) = Intercept
  • \( \alpha_{i} \), \( \beta_{t} \) = State & quarter FE
  • \( \tau \) = ATT
  • \( D_{it} \) = 1 if passed extended payment plans, 0 otherwise
  • \( \hat{w}_{i} \), \( \hat{\lambda}_{t} \) = Unit & time weights

Source: Arkhangelsky et al. (2021).

* To identify treatment effects of the extended payment plans, I use synthetic DiD model. At first glance, it looks very similar to DiD model but with two extra terms in the end. * Let me quickly go through some key notations first in this equation: * \( Y_{it} \) is the financial health in state i, quarter t * \( \mu \) is intercept. * \( \alpha_{i} \), \(\beta_{t} \) are state, quarter fixed effects * \( \tau \) is our interest ATT or average treatment effet on the treated * \( D_{it} \) is treatment variable, D = 1 if passed extended payment plans, 0 otherwise * \( w_{i} \) and $\lambda_{t}$ are unit and time weights, which I will introduce more in a bit. * T is total time or quarters, here $T = 24$ since 6 years have 24 quarters. * So what are unit and time weights exactly? What do they do?

SDiD: Weights

    \[ Unit: \hat{w}_{i} = argmin\| \bar{y}_{pre, tr} - (w_{0} + Y_{pre, co}w_{co})\|_{2}^{2} + \zeta T_{pre}\|w_{co}\|^2_{2} \]
  • Match pre-treatment trends between control & treated units
  • Parallel trend → exploits advantages of DiD & SC
    • \[ Time: \hat{\lambda}_{t} = argmin\| \bar{y}_{post, co} - (\lambda_{0} + \lambda_{pre}Y_{pre, co} + \lambda_{0})\|_{2}^{2} \]
  • Balance pre- & post-treatment periods for control units
  • Increase precision of treatment effect estimates


Notes: Unit weights in a simpler term: \( \sum_{i=1}^{N_{co}} \hat{w}_{i}Y_{it} \approx N_{tr}^{-1}\sum_{i=N_{co}+1}^{N}Y_{it} \) for all \( t = 1, 2, ..., T_{pre} \). Time weights in simpler term, \( \sum_{t=1}^{T_{pre}}\lambda_{t}Y_{it}\approx T_{post}^{-1}\sum_{T_{pre}+1}Y_{it} \) for all \( i=1, 2, ..., N_{co} \).

* Simply put: Unit weights match pre-treatment trends between treated & control units. * Another way to show how the unit weights work mathematically is the equation in the notes. Basically, the unit weights minimize the difference between weighted average of pre-treated outcome of control units and pre-treated outcome of treated units. * This is similar to what synthetic control does, except that we will have parallel trend because we included an intercept term $w_{0}$ here. * On the other hand, the time weights are used to balance pre-treatment and post-treatment periods for control units. * Another way to show how the time weights work mathematically is the equation in the notes. Basically, the times weights minimize the difference between weighted average of pre-treated outcome of control units and post-treated outcome of control units.

SDiD: Staggered Adoption

    \[ \frac{8}{65}ATT_{DE} + \frac{5}{65}ATT_{FL} + \frac{22}{65}ATT_{LA} + \frac{13}{65}ATT_{NV} + \frac{17}{65}ATT_{UT} \]
  • Run synthetic DiD for each treated state
  • Get 5 ATTs
  • Re-weight ATT: by ratio of a state's treated vs total treated periods



Source: Clarke et al. (2023).

Because SDID requires a clear definition of the pre-treatment period and control units, we have to run SDID for each treated state, so we get 5 ATTs, then we get the final ATT by weighting each ATT by the ratio of a state's treated vs total treated periods. For example, the sum of treated periods for the 5 states is 65, so that's the denominator. Deleware was treated for 8 states, so we reweight ATT of Deleware by $\frac{8}{65}$. Do the same for the rest of treated states and sum them up.

  • Experience volatile income:
    • No access to traditional credit
    • Or fear to jeopardize credit
    • Resort to payday loans → "predatory"
    • Extended payment plans
You may wonder: why not they just use traditional banks such as credit card to borrow money? Well, we just saw that 4.5% of US population are "unbanked", which means they don't have a bank account, so they don't have access to traditional credit. For those who do have bank accounts, they may fear jeopardizing their credit scores by borrowing from banks. So they resort to payday loan, which doesn't require checking credit scores. Given how "predatory" the payday loan is, some banks passed extended payment plans to protect these consumers.

I'll also skip information about Clarity data and the extended payment plans legal status. What I'd like to add is the definition of income volatility. There are many ways to quantify income volatility. In my research, I decided to use income coefficient of variation or here I call it income CV, which is used in various papers, such as Hannagan and Morduch (2015), which uses the US Financial Diaries data. We get income CV by diviging the standard deviation of income with mean of income and multiply by 100%. So income CV will always be positive, which means we focus on the magnitude of the changes in income, not the change of direction. And the larger the values are, the more volatile the income. The final data is aggregated at yearly level for each borrower for each year. So there will be 6 separate sample data (2015~2020). In 2015, there're about 1500 borrowers in treated states & 4 thousand in control states. The rest of year the borrowers may increase or decrease a bit.

Model: DML (ATE)

    \[ y = g_{0}(D, X) + \zeta,\ E[\zeta|X, D] = 0 \] \[ D = m_{0}(X) + \nu,\ E[\nu|X] = 0 \] \[ ATE = E[g_{0}(1, X) - g_{0}(0, X)] \]
  • \( g_{0}(\cdot) \) = Nonlinear function (ML model)
  • \( y \) = Financial health
  • \( D = 1 \) if borrower lives in treated state, 0 otherwise
  • \( X \) = Borrower features
  • \( m_{0}(\cdot) \) = Nonlinear function (ML model)

Source: Chetverikov et al. (2016) and Chernozhukov et al (2018).

To estimate conditional average treatment effect or CATE, I'll need to estimate ATE. The model I'll be using is double machine learning, or I call it DML here. [more later]

Model: DML (ATE)

  • Use 5-fold cross-validation
  • Use random forest (ML) to get estimators for each fold
    • Maximum depth: 5
    • Minimum sample leaf: 2
  • ATE: average the five estimators


Source: Chetverikov et al. (2016) and Chernozhukov et al (2018).

Since double machine learning uses both econometrics & machine learning techniques, we'll introduce some machine learning methods to avoid overfitting. If the model overfits, it'll give us very poor estimates in the end. Here we'll use 5-fold cross validation, a pretty common setting. I use random forest model for its model efficiency and great prediction performance. For each fold, the maximum depth is set at 5 and minimum sample leaf at 2. [may put these details in extra slidese in the end] Then we average the 5-fold estimators to get a final ATE.

To get CATE, I use the best-linear-predictor of the linear score following Semenova and Chernozhukov (2021). We have to define a data frame of basis functions to approximate the target function g(x) with a linear form. Here, we use B-splines with quadratic form and a degree of freedom of 5 using sample code by Microsoft.

Here's the visualized results for CATE for variable amount past due in each year. The overall trend in each year appears to be U-shaped: people who are in between the first quartile and median income volatility benefit the most from this law; borrowers at the first 25th percentile gradually receive more and more treatment effect; people between median and third quartile receive less and less treatment effect until the effect disappears; after the third quartile, people do not seem to receive any treatment effect overall. Second, people whose income volatility is below 75% percentile generally do receive a reduction in their amount past due because of extended payment plans. Finally, the treatment effect varies from year to year, and there seems to be no apparent pattern (i.e., if the treatment effect increases or decreases over the years).

Conclusions & Comments

  • Conclusion:
    • Treatment effect varies from year to year, no clear pattern
    • Between first quartile and median: most benefit
    • Beyond third quartile: little to no benefit
  • Comments:
    • Income CV invariant to scales or absolute changes in income
    • Not show income dynamics over time
    • Future work: use other income volatility measurements
I'll just add some comments on top of the results I just presented: The income CV used in this chapter is invariant to scales or absolute changes in income. So it won't show income dynamics over time, for example, whether the income volatility moves toward a positive direction or negative direction. The future work could introduce other types of income volatility measures that measure how frequent the income changes are and the directions of the changes.
