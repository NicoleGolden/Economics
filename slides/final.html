In Chapter 1, I'd like to analyze the milk consumption patterns among US migrants.

Motivation: Why Milk Consumption?

  • Plant milk: partly contribute to the decline
    • Price premium

Source: USDA (2022), Loh, Seah and Looi (2021), and Foods Association (2022).

So why milk consumption? According to USDA, there has been a consistent decline in dairy milk consumption. In 1970, the daily per capita consumption of dairy milk was about 1 cup. By 2019, the daily per capita consumption reduced to half. Plant-based milk partly contributes to the decline, despite its price premium. People may consume plant-based milk as a nutrient substitute, and it's considered more environmentally friendly and better for animal welfare. During COVID-19, plant-based milk became more trendy for reasons such as health or change of lifestyle. This makes the study of consumption for both types of milk important.

Research Question

  • How does migration to another state affect dairy & plant milk consumption?
    • Expenditure patterns
    • Geographic variations
  • Contribution:
    • Research gaps → quantify
    • Dietary policies: WIC
And this brings me to my research question: How does migration to another state affect dairy & plant-based milk consumption? Specifically, my research uncovers the expenditure patterns for both types of milk, and learns how the new local environment affects HH’s expenditures. This research fills in the current research gaps and quantifies these questions. It also has dietary-related policy implications, especially the policy for the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, and Children, known as WIC.

Background: Dairy vs Plant Milk

Source: Plant Based Foods Association (2020), Gerliani, Hammami and Aider (2019), and Boaitey and Minegishi (2020).
Image source: author.

Here's a comparison of dairy and plant-based milk. In terms of sales growth in 2019, dairy milk experienced only a 0.1% increase, whereas it's 5% for plant-based milk. Traditional dairy milk contains lactose. It can be made lactose-free of course. But plant-based milk is inherently lactose-free. Dairy milk is a type of conventional beverage because it's been around since the 19th century, whereas plant-based milk is a type of functional beverage. It's designed not only to satisfy our desire for a milk-like taste but also our nutritional needs. Lastly, plant-based milk has many other advantages in this modern world: such as lower environmental impact, as you can see from the figures here. Its production doesn't involve animals; it satisfies a flexitarian lifestyle, so if you're vegan or allergic to dairy milk, plant-based milk is a more suitable alternative. During COVID-19, many people also started to consume plant-based milk instead of just dairy milk.

DID Model: Geographic Variations

    \[ ln(y)_{it} = \alpha_{i} + \tau_{t} + \beta \cdot \Delta_{i} \cdot D_{it} + X^\prime_{it}\Gamma + \epsilon_{it} \]
  • \( \Delta_{i} = \bar{y}_{N,i} - \bar{y}_{O,i} \)
    • Size of move
    • Diff between average milk expenditures in mover's new state vs old state
  • \( \beta \) = Our interest
    • Measures how much milk expenditures converge toward new states’ average

Source: Finkelstein et al. (2016).

This DiD model looks similar to the standard DiD model earlier but with an added term. The \( \Delta_{it} \) term here is called "size of move", which is the difference between average milk expenditures in the mover's new state vs old state. By interacting "size of move" with treatment variable, we get \( \beta \), our interest. It measures how much the milk expenditures converge toward the new states’ average.

Note 1: This model uses movers data only.
Note 2: "size of move" is calculated non-movers data only. To compute state-level average outcomes: first average across HHs in each quarter and each state using sample weights (and info on the number of children), then take an average across time periods. You can simply think of this as expenditure of self regress on expenditure difference of others (who are in your new or old states))
Note 3: \( \beta \) measures the fraction of difference between new state and old state that has been covered. This way, the size of the jump after the move measures the share of the average difference between areas that is attributable to the current environment.)

Why should we care about financial health? According to Financial Health Network, an NGO that has been studying the financial health of Americans for the past decade, about 67% of Americans are not financially healthy. Let me explain. Financial Health Network used the University of Southern California survey data of more than 6 thousand respondents, and found that, on average, 37% of individuals spend more than their income or can't pay bills on time, 47% don't have sufficient liquid savings or long-term savings, 38% have unmanageable debt or non-prime credit, and 42% don't have appropriate insurance or don't plan ahead financially. We can see that poor financial health in America is still prevalent.

Motivation: Payday loans

In my study, I'd like to focus on the group who have non-prime credits. The non-prime consumers tend to take out payday loans. According to CFPB, there are 12 million payday loan users per year. People are attracted to borrow payday loans because they are typically small-dollar, short-term, and unsecured. However, payday loans are very costly. Typically the APRs are between 300 to 500%. As of 2020, 22 states either banned or effectively banned payday loans, because they see payday loans as predatory loans. Among the rest of the states where payday loans are still legal, 15 states passed so-called extended payment plans to protect payday loan borrowers.

Research Question

  • Do extended payment plans improve financial health?
  • Wang and Burke (2022):
    • Texas (2012): disclosure comparing cost of payday loans vs other credit products
    • Significant decline in payday loan volume
  • Contribution:
    • Broader payday loan policy applied to more states
In my research, I'd like to answer this question: Do extended payment plans improve financial health? Wang and Burke (2022) did a similar research. They studied the effects of information disclosure in Texas starting in 2012. The disclosure compares the cost of payday loans vs other credit products, and presents their likelihood of renewal in simple terms. They found that the disclosures have led to a significant and persistent decline in payday loan volume. In my study, the extended payment plan is a much broader payday loan policy since they typically require more than information disclosure, and it's applied to multiple states instead of just one state.

Background: Payday Loans

  • Legal in 29 states (2020), loan limit $500
  • Unsecured, no credit check, repay in next payday
  • High APRs: 300% ~ 500% (credit cards: 12% ~ 30%)
  • Features: "cash, quick, fast, easy, emergency bills"
  • Rollover: charges may double! → "debt traps"


Source: CFPB (2021).

Let me introduce a bit more about payday loan. It's legal in 29 states as of 2020. It's a small-dollar loan with a typical loan amount of $500. It's unsecured because it doesn't require any collateral, and it doesn't require a formal credit check. Consumers repay on their next payday. Compared with only 12~30% of APR for credit cards, the payday loan has a much higher APR, typically between 300~500%. Based on advertisements I scrapped from the top 10 payday loan lender's websites, the payday loan is typically featured as being "quick cash, fast, easy, emergency bills." If a user opts in a rollover, which means they choose to pay back on the next payday instead of this one, the charges may double. This feature keeps some consumers in "debt traps."

Background:
Extended Payment Plans

  • Key features:
    • Repay in 3 ~ 4 installments (salient) with zero charges
    • Minimum repayment term 60 ~ 90 days
    • Limit use to once a year
    • Eligible only if reach a threshold of rollovers
    • Require lenders to disclose availability of the plans

Source: CFPB (2022).

To protect consumers, some states have passed extended payment plans. These plans have the following features:
Users can choose to repay in 3~4 installments. This is the most salient feature of the plans. And they can repay with zero additional charges. There is a minimum repayment term typically between 60~90 days. The plans may be limited to use once a year. Consumers may only be eligible if they reach a threshold of rollovers. The lenders may be required to disclose the availability of the extended payment plans.

Model: SDiD

    \[ \hat{\tau}^{SDID} = argmin_{\mu, \alpha, \beta, \tau} \Bigl\{\sum_{i = 1}^{N} \sum_{t = 1}^{T} (Y_{it} - \mu - \alpha_{i} - \beta_{t} - \tau D_{it})^{2} \hat{w}_{i} \hat{\lambda}_{t} \Bigl\} \]
  • \( Y_{it} \) = Financial health in state \(i \), quarter \(t \)
  • \( \mu \) = Intercept
  • \( \alpha_{i} \), \( \beta_{t} \) = State & quarter FE
  • \( \tau \) = ATT
  • \( D_{it} \) = Post_Plans
  • \( \hat{w}_{i} \), \( \hat{\lambda}_{t} \) = Unit & time weights

Source: Arkhangelsky et al. (2021).

To identify the treatment effects of the extended payment plans, I use a synthetic DiD model, or I call it SDID here. At first glance, it looks very similar to DiD model but with two extra terms at the end.
Let me quickly go through some key notations in this equation:
\( Y_{it} \) is the financial health variable in state i, quarter t
\( \mu \) is the intercept
\( \alpha_{i} \) and \(\beta_{t} \) are the state and quarter FE
\( \tau \) is our interest, the average treatment effect on the treated, or ATT
\( D_{it} \) is the treatment variable
\( w_{i} \) and \( \lambda_{t} \) are unit and time weights, which I will introduce more later.
The capital N is total units or states, so here \( N = 19 \) since we have 14 control states and 5 treated states.
And capital T is total time or quarters, so here \( T = 24 \) since in 6 years we have 24 quarters.

SDiD: Weights

    \[ Unit: \hat{w}_{i} = argmin\| \bar{y}_{pre, tr} - (w_{0} + Y_{pre, co}w_{co})\|_{2}^{2} + \zeta T_{pre}\|w_{co}\|^2_{2} \]
  • Match pre-treatment trends between control & treated units
  • Parallel trend → exploits advantages of DiD & SC
    • \[ Time: \hat{\lambda}_{t} = argmin\| \bar{y}_{post, co} - (\lambda_{0} + \lambda_{pre}Y_{pre, co} + \lambda_{0})\|_{2}^{2} \]
  • Balance pre- & post-treatment periods for control units
  • Increase precision of treatment effect estimates


Notes: Unit weights in a simpler term: \( \sum_{i=1}^{N_{co}} \hat{w}_{i}Y_{it} \approx N_{tr}^{-1}\sum_{i=N_{co}+1}^{N}Y_{it} \) for all \( t = 1, 2, ..., T_{pre} \). Time weights in simpler term, \( \sum_{t=1}^{T_{pre}}\lambda_{t}Y_{it}\approx T_{post}^{-1}\sum_{T_{pre}+1}Y_{it} \) for all \( i=1, 2, ..., N_{co} \).

So what are unit and time weights exactly? What do they do?
Simply put: Unit weights match the pre-treatment trends between treated & control units. Another way to show how the unit weights work mathematically is the equation in the notes. Basically, the unit weights minimize the difference between the weighted average of the pre-treated outcome of control units and the pre-treated outcome of treated units.
This is similar to what synthetic control does, except that we will have a parallel trend because we included an intercept term \( w_{0} \) here.
The time weights here are used to balance pre-treatment and post-treatment periods for control units. Another way to show how the time weights work mathematically is the equation in the notes. Basically, the times weights minimize the difference between the weighted average of the pre-treated outcome of control units and the post-treated outcome of control units.

SDiD: Staggered Adoption

    \[ \frac{8}{65}ATT_{DE} + \frac{5}{65}ATT_{FL} + \frac{22}{65}ATT_{LA} + \frac{13}{65}ATT_{NV} + \frac{17}{65}ATT_{UT} \]
  • Run synthetic DiD for each treated state
  • Get 5 ATTs
  • Re-weight ATT: by ratio of a state's treated vs total treated periods



Source: Clarke et al. (2023).

Because synthetic DID requires a clear definition of the pre-treatment period and control units, we have to run the SDID model for each treated state, so we get 5 ATTs. Then we get the final ATT by re-weighting each ATT by the ratio of a state's treated periods vs total treated periods. For example, the total treated periods for the 5 states is 65, so that's the denominator. Deleware was treated for 8 quarters, so we need to reweight the ATT of Deleware by \( \frac{8}{65} \). And we do the same for Florida, Louisiana, Nevada, and Utah. The final ATT will be the sum.

Motivation: The Lows & Highs

Data source: Clarity 2015 data. Note: “Low income” if an individual’s mean income is below the third quartile of $3,524, and “high income” is above $3,524. “Low volatility” if an individual’s income CV is below 75%, and “high volatility” if it is above 75%.

I'll also skip the Clarity data and the extended payment plans legal status. What I'd like to add here is the definition of income volatility. There are many ways to quantify income volatility. In my research, I decided to use the income coefficient of variation, or I just call it income CV. We get income CV by dividing the standard deviation of income by the mean of income and multiplying by 100%. So income CV will always be positive, It indicates the magnitude of the changes in income and covers the changes of income in both directions, positive or negative. Model: DML (ATE)

    \[ (1) \ y = g_{0}(D, X) + \zeta \] \[ (2) \ D = m_{0}(X) + \nu \] \[ (3) \ \tau = E[g_{0}(1, X) - g_{0}(0, X)] \]
  • \( g_{0}(\cdot) \), \( m_{0}(\cdot) \) = Nonlinear ML model
  • \( y \) = Financial health
  • \( D \) = Post_Plans
  • \( X \) = Borrowers' features
  • \( \tau \) = ATE

Source: Chetverikov et al. (2016) and Chernozhukov et al (2018).

To estimate conditional average treatment effect or CATE, I'll need to estimate ATE first. The model I'll be using is double machine learning, or I call it DML. The first equation is our main equation: which estimates the treatment effect, \( \tau \), which is the difference between mean treated outcomes and control outcomes, shown in equation 3. The second equation keeps track of the dependence of the treatment variable D on controls X. This equation is not our main interest, but it is important for removing regularization bias. The confounding factors X affect the policy variable D through function \( m_{0}(X) \) and the outcome variable through function \( g_{0}(X) \).
So the functions \( g_{0}(\cdot) \) and \( m_{0}(\cdot) \) are some non-linear machine learning model, which I'll introduce more later. Y is the financial health variable, D is treatment variable, X is a set of borrowers' features such as age, housing status. And \( \tau \) is the ATE we want to identify.

Model: DML (ATE)

    \[ (1) \ y = g_{0}(D, X) + \zeta \] \[ (2) \ D = m_{0}(X) + \nu \]
  • Similar to FWL theorem:
    • Step 1: reg \( y \) ~ \( X \), reg \( D \) ~ \( X \), get residualized \( \tilde{y} \), \( \tilde{D} \)
    • Step 2: reg \( \tilde{y} \) ~ \( \tilde{D} \), get \( \tau \)
  • Random forest:
    • 5-fold cross-validation, average estimates


Source: Chetverikov et al. (2016) and Chernozhukov et al (2018).

The double machine learning works very similarly to the FWL theorem. It works in 2 steps: first, run 2 separate regressions using some non-linear machine learning models, regress outcome y on features X, get residualized y; and regress treatment D on features X, get residualized D. Second, regress residualized y on residualized D to get treatment effect \( \tau \).
In step 1, we don't really care much about the coefficients on X. We just want to take advantage of the machine learning model's prediction power to get good predictions of y and D. In residualized y and D: all variance due to X is explained way. That's how in step 2, we can get the causal effect \( \tau \).
The machine learning I'll use is random forest. To avoid overfitting, we'll use 5-fold cross-validation, which is a pretty common setting. I use random forest because of its efficiency and great prediction performance. The final ATE is the average of the 5-fold estimates.

Conclusions & Comments

    • Between first quartile and median: most benefit
    • Beyond third quartile: little to no benefit
  • Comments:
    • Income CV invariant to scales or absolute changes in income
    • Not show income dynamics over time
    • Future work: use other income volatility measurements
I'll just add some comments on top of the results I just presented: The income CV used in this chapter is invariant to scales or absolute changes in income. So it won't show income dynamics over time, for example, whether the income volatility moves toward a positive or negative direction. Future work could introduce other types of income volatility measurements that can show how frequent the income changes are and the directions of the changes. And maybe they can lead to some other interesting findings.
